Answer to the Millennium interview question¶

NOTE: The full repository (including data, custom functions, and this notebook) is available on GitHub: https://github.com/lucien-sim/electricity-demand-forecast.

The problem¶

Predict electricity loads on a certain day in September 2020 using 60 years of station observations from many different stations in the region, and a model that estimates electricity loads from those observations (among other factors).

For the purposes of the problem, I'll interpret the "electricity load" as the total demand for electricity over an entire day (in MWh; EIA predicts this quantity here). For example, if the demand in a certain area was 10 MWh for every hour in an entire day, then the "load" for that day would be 24*10 MWh = 240 MWh. This is the quantity I'll try to predict.

My response¶

Providing an accurate, deterministic forecast will be impossible because electricity loads are strongly dependent on weather and weather can't be predicted that far out. Therefore, I will give a probabilistic forecast that includes an "expected" load, as well as reasonable upper/lower bounds for the load.

Strategy for deriving the "expected" load and bounds:

  1. Plug the weather observations (and other inputs) into the model. This will provide estimates of what the electricity load would have been on every day in the past 60 years, if grid infrastructure had always been the same as it is now.
  2. Then compile the daily electricity loads during the two weeks centered on that day in September, for every year in those 60 years of estimated loads (do this separately for weekdays and weekends). This forms a distribution of what's reasonable to expect for that day in September 2020, if that day falls on either a weekend or weekday. I would use the two-week window so that the distribution is well-enough populated to get good estimates of the upper and lower bounds, but limit the window width to two weeks so that changes in climatology are small over the window.
  3. For my estimates of the lower bound, expected load, and upper bound, I would give the 1st percentile, median, and 99th percentile of the weekend/weekday load distribution.

Taking climate change into account: I would adjust the weather observations so that they're consistent with the expected climate state in 2020. For example, if I found that temperatures were consistently rising by 0.05 degC/yr in the region of interest, I'd add the following amount to the temperature observations in each year, $yr$: $0.05*(2020-yr)$. This would remove the upward temperature trend by raising temperature measurements from previous years. I wouldn't adjust the relative humidity, cloud cover, or wind observations because climate change-associated changes in those quantities are probably more difficult to pin down.

Model for estimating electricity loads¶

Inputs: Day of week, hour of day, temperature (degC), and relative humidity (%).

Output: hourly electricity demand (in MWh).

The model takes the following form:

$$LOAD = a(RH)*b(DOW)*c(HOD)*f(T),$$

where $a(RH)$ is a coefficient that depends on relative humidity, $b(DOW)$ is a coefficient that depends on the day of week, $c(HOD)$ is a coefficient that depends on the hour of day, and $f(T)$ is a value that depends on the temperature. These coefficients can be calculated from the relative humidity ($RH$), day of week ($DOW$), and hour of day ($HOD$) according to the following equations:

$$ a(RH) = \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} 1+0.05*\frac{(rh-50)}{50}, & RH\ge50 \\ 1, & RH<50 \\ \end{array} \right. $$

$$ b(DOW) = \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} 1.0, & DOW \in (M,T,W,R,F) \\ 0.9, & DOW \in (Sa,Su) \\ \end{array} \right. $$

$$c(HOD) = 1-0.125\cos[(HOD-4)*\pi/12]$$$$ f(T) = \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} 4000-1000*\frac{T-15}{15}, & T<15 \\ 4000+8*(T-15)^2, & T\ge15 \\ \end{array} \right. $$

# Load estimator function: 
def load_estimator(row):
    RHc = lambda rh: 1+0.05*(rh-50)/50 if rh>=50 else 1
    DOWc = lambda dow: 1 if dow >=1 and dow<=5 else 0.9
    HODc = lambda hod: -0.125*np.cos((hod-4)*np.pi/12)+1
    temp2load = lambda T: (4000-(1000*(T-15)/15)) if T<15 else (4000+10*(T-15)**2)
    return RHc(row['relative_humidity'])*DOWc(row['day_of_week'])*HODc(row['hour'])*\
            temp2load(row['air_temp'])

obs_df['load'] = obs_df.apply(load_estimator,axis=1)
fig = plot_load_estimations(obs_df['20180901':'20180930'])

Calculate electricity forecasts for every day of the year in 2020¶

How the forecasts are made for a single day (August 14, for example):

  1. I compile daily electricity load distributions for days that fall between August 7th and August 21st, during each year of observations. I do this separately for the days that fall on weekdays and weekends.
  2. I then find the median, 1st, 99th, 5th, and 95th percentiles of this "historical" distribution. These numbers make up my forecast.

The top plot shows the anticipated weekday demands, while the bottom plot shows the anticipated weekend demands. On both plots, the red line represents the "expected" (median) demand, while the boundaries of the dark gray shading represent the 5th and 95th percentiles, and the boundaries of the light gray shading represent the 1st and 99th percentiles.

If someone asked me to forecast the electricity demand for September 2nd, 2020 (a Tuesday), I'd say that I expect the demand to be around 115,000 MWh (the median of the weekday distribution for that day), but say that the demand could fall anywhere between 100,000 MWh and 150,000 MWh (the approximate 1st and 99th percentiles).
